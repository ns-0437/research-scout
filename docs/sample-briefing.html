Otter.ai business tier pricing

Competitor — Priya flagged that Otter.ai recently changed pricing and the team needs current numbers to set competitive Q3 pricing.

Otter.ai's Business tier is priced at $30 per user per month billed monthly, or roughly $20 per user per month billed annually (the annual rate is often listed precisely as $19.99). This is consistent across multiple recent sources: Otter Business costs $30 per user per month (or $20 per month when billed annually), and it's designed for teams, offering collaborative workspaces, team-wide access to transcripts, live captions for meetings, and admin controls. A May 2026 review of the official pricing page frames it similarly: Otter.ai Business costs $30/user/month (monthly) or $19.99/user/month (annual), with unlimited meeting & in-app recording transcription, up to 6,000 imported-file minutes/user/month, and a 4-hour per-conversation cap. Note that annual billing represents roughly a 33% discount versus monthly on this tier.

On features, the Business plan layers team and admin capabilities on top of everything in Pro. Key inclusions: admin controls with usage analytics and prioritized support; concurrent meeting support that joins up to 3 concurrent virtual meetings to automatically write and share notes; and expanded usage limits of 6,000 monthly transcription minutes, 4 hours per conversation, and unlimited audio or video file imports. It also adds team collaboration tooling — it includes everything in the Pro plan plus team collaboration tools, and with Otter's Business plan you get 6,000 minutes of transcription per user per month.

One important watch-out for your competitive analysis: there's a genuine discrepancy in how sources describe the Business minute allowance. Most list it as 6,000 minutes per user/month, but at least one source reads the current pricing page as giving Business unlimited meeting transcription with the 6,000-minute figure applying only to imported file minutes. This distinction matters if you're benchmarking value-per-dollar, so I'd verify it directly on otter.ai/pricing before quoting it externally. Also worth flagging for Priya's point about the recent change: the reported shift wasn't primarily to the Business tier but to Pro — Otter quietly cut the Pro plan from 6,000 to 1,200 transcription minutes without lowering the price, which several reviewers now cite as the notable 2025–2026 pricing move. Finally, sales-specific AI features (OtterPilot for Sales) and SSO/HIPAA compliance are reserved for Enterprise, not Business, per the Enterprise tier being the only one with sales-specific AI features — relevant if you're positioning your own tiers.

One caveat on sourcing: nearly all figures above come from competitor/review blogs (tl;dv, Sonix, Claap, Notta, etc.) rather than Otter's own page, since the live pricing page returned limited scrapeable detail. The dollar figures are highly consistent across independent sources, so I'm confident in the $30/$20 pricing, but I'd confirm the exact feature limits on the official page before finalizing any published comparison.

Per-seat vs usage-based pricing trend

Claim to verify — Marcus claimed AI meeting assistants are moving to per-seat pricing and the team agreed to verify before committing.

Marcus is right for the meeting-assistant category specifically, even though the broader AI-SaaS narrative is moving the other way. The leading AI meeting-notes products—Otter, Fireflies, Fathom, tl;dv, Read AI, and Granola—are all sold on a per-user, per-month basis rather than metered usage. The prices published for 2025 are consistently per-seat: Fathom's paid tiers are priced at roughly $15–$29/user/month on annual billing: Premium ~$15/user/month (annual) or $19/user/month (monthly), Team Edition ~$19/user/month, Team Edition Pro ~$29/user/month, and Granola's Basic plan is free forever but limits meeting history and excludes integrations, with paid plans starting at $14/user/month. Where these vendors introduce "usage," it appears as a per-seat allowance cap, not consumption billing—e.g., Otter's Pro plan raises the monthly cap to 1,200 transcription minutes with a 90-minute per-conversation limit, and Business brings that to 6,000 minutes per month. So the right mental model for this niche is "per-seat with a metered ceiling," which is closer to seat pricing than to pay-as-you-go.

The reason this category resists the usage/outcome shift is structural: there is no clean billable unit of value for a summarizer. One analysis puts it bluntly—outcome pricing for "AI that helps with strategy" or "AI that summarizes meetings" doesn't really work — there's no clean unit to charge for. Meeting notes are treated as a productivity multiplier sitting alongside humans, which is exactly the profile where per-seat still fits: for AI agents that augment human work — like a Copilot-style assistant — per-seat still makes sense, and per-seat wins when your AI is a productivity multiplier sitting next to humans, not replacing them, with usage volume per user roughly predictable and customers wanting budget predictability.

That said, be careful how you frame "per-seat vs. usage-based" internally, because the wider AI market is genuinely moving—just not toward pure usage. The dominant direction is hybrid (per-seat base plus a usage/credit component): per-seat pricing is not dead, but roughly 65% of SaaS vendors are now layering usage-based components on top of it, and hybrid models report the highest median growth rate at 21%, outperforming pure subscription and pure usage models. One tracker reports ChatGPT Business, ChatGPT Enterprise, Claude Team, Cursor Pro and Perplexity Pro all sell per-seat with prices stable on a published table, while the OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google APIs sell usage-based, quoted per million tokens—i.e., seat pricing dominates the end-user "assistant" layer while token metering lives at the API layer. Note that several of these data points come from vendor/consultancy blogs (Bain, Bessemer, Maxio figures cited secondhand), and the exact percentages vary between them, so treat the precise numbers as directional rather than authoritative.

For your Q3 decision: per-seat is the category norm and the safest primary model for a meeting-notes SaaS, and the free-tier gating done via minute caps (Otter caps free users at 300 minutes per month and Fireflies limits total free storage to 800 minutes) is the standard way competitors add a soft usage lever without full usage-based billing. The thing to watch is that "predominantly per-seat" is increasingly "per-seat plus a metered cap or credit bucket," so consider building an overage/allowance mechanism into your plans rather than a pure flat seat. Stripe Billing vs Paddle for EU VAT

Tool / vendor — The merchant-of-record and EU VAT handling question between Stripe Billing and Paddle was left open before launch.

The two platforms sit on opposite sides of the "merchant of record" (MoR) line, and that distinction is the whole ballgame for EU VAT.

Paddle is a full merchant of record. Legally, Paddle acts as a reseller of your product, and is therefore the "seller on record," so Paddle is responsible for the collection and payment of VAT and tax instead of you. In practice that means Paddle entirely handles sales tax for all digital products where it's a legal requirement — including EU VAT — and because it's registered in over 100 jurisdictions it collects customer info, charges the correct VAT, remits it to the relevant authorities, and issues compliant invoices. Critically for risk, this model ensures your sales are always tax-compliant, and all the tax-related risk rests with Paddle, not with you. Paddle also handles EU B2B correctly: if a VAT-registered business buys through Paddle cross-border, they enter a valid VAT ID at checkout and don't pay VAT to Paddle; instead the buying business becomes responsible for reporting the VAT via the reverse-charge mechanism in its own country.

Stripe Billing is a payment processor, not a merchant of record — you remain the seller of record and the party legally responsible for VAT. The key distinction is that Stripe is a payment processor: you handle tax compliance, VAT collection, and sales tax filings yourself, and while Stripe Tax helps it doesn't fully solve this. Stripe's add-on, Stripe Tax, handles VAT calculation and reporting for you, but you still need to actually remit your taxes — that isn't done for you as it is with a merchant of record, so you'll likely need to spend your own time or hire someone to file. Concretely, with Stripe you are responsible for registering for VAT in every EU country where you have customers, tracking tax rates, filing returns, and handling audits. (Note: Stripe has a newer "Managed Payments" MoR-style offering — it provides some simplification for tax and compliance but still requires users to manage much of their payment stack — but that is separate from standard Stripe Billing and was in beta as of these sources, so I'd treat it as not-yet-proven for your launch.)

A few things the team should weigh before deciding. Cost: the MoR convenience isn't free — Paddle charges 5% + $0.50 per transaction, versus Stripe's standard 2.9% + $0.30, though once you add Stripe Tax (~0.5%) plus international card fees and the labor/cost of actually filing returns, Stripe's effective rate can creep up close to 5% anyway. Threshold nuance: MoR value scales with your international footprint — one practitioner notes MoR earns its 5% once you're genuinely multi-region and past the €10k EU cross-border threshold; below that you can charge home-country VAT and self-file, so the tax argument is weaker than it looks. And two MoR trade-offs specific to a subscription business like yours: because Paddle is the seller, your buyers see Paddle on the invoice rather than you, which can complicate B2B customers who want to reverse-charge and reclaim VAT against your name, and you carry offboarding risk since you're not the merchant — plus MoR lock-in includes the customer relationship, since payment methods don't transfer if you leave. Net: if you want to launch by end of Q3 without standing up EU VAT registration and filing, Paddle removes that burden entirely; if you'd rather keep lower fees, the direct customer/invoice relationship, and full billing control — and are willing to own VAT filing (with Stripe Tax assisting) — Stripe Billing is the path, ideally after checking whether you're even over the EU thresholds yet.

Sources conflict slightly on exact effective costs (they depend heavily on your customer mix and volume), so I'd model your own numbers before committing rather than relying on the headline percentages.
